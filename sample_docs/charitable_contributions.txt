IRS Publication 526 - Charitable Contributions

CHARITABLE CONTRIBUTION LIMITS

For tax year 2024, you can generally deduct charitable contributions up to 60% of your adjusted gross income (AGI). However, different limits apply depending on the type of property donated and the type of organization receiving the donation.

CASH CONTRIBUTIONS
- 60% AGI limit for cash contributions to public charities
- 30% AGI limit for cash contributions to private foundations
- Contributions exceeding these limits can be carried forward for up to 5 years

PROPERTY CONTRIBUTIONS
- 30% AGI limit for appreciated capital gain property to public charities
- 20% AGI limit for appreciated property to private foundations
- Fair market value is generally used for property held more than one year

QUALIFIED CHARITABLE DISTRIBUTIONS (QCDs)
Individuals age 70Â½ or older can make qualified charitable distributions of up to $100,000 per year directly from their IRA to a qualified charity. QCDs count toward your required minimum distribution (RMD) but are not included in taxable income.

SUBSTANTIATION REQUIREMENTS

Cash Contributions:
- Under $250: Bank record, receipt, or written communication from charity
- $250 or more: Written acknowledgment from the charity showing date, amount, and whether goods/services were provided

Non-Cash Contributions:
- Under $250: Receipt from charity with description
- $250 to $500: Written acknowledgment from charity
- Over $500: Form 8283 required
- Over $5,000: Qualified appraisal required (with exceptions for publicly traded securities)

QUALIFIED ORGANIZATIONS
Contributions are deductible only if made to qualified organizations including:
- Churches and religious organizations
- Nonprofit educational organizations
- Nonprofit hospitals and medical research organizations
- Public charities described in section 501(c)(3)
- Federal, state, and local governments (for public purposes)

NON-DEDUCTIBLE CONTRIBUTIONS
You cannot deduct contributions to:
- Individuals
- Political organizations or candidates
- Social clubs, labor unions, or chambers of commerce
- Foreign organizations (with limited exceptions)
- Organizations operated for personal profit

VOLUNTEER EXPENSES
You may deduct unreimbursed out-of-pocket expenses when serving as a volunteer:
- Travel expenses (actual costs or 14 cents per mile for 2024)
- Cost of uniforms required by the organization
- Supplies purchased for the organization's use

SPECIAL RULES FOR CLOTHING AND HOUSEHOLD ITEMS
Donated clothing and household items must be in good used condition or better to be deductible. Items with minimal value are not deductible. A single item valued over $500 requires a qualified appraisal.
